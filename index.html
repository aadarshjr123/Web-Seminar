2. CARs model and IMRaD Model

2.1. CARS Model : Create a Research Space

The introduction is the most important part of any scientific topic or paper because it helps the readers to understand the particular paper without reading the whole context. So basically by following the CARS model we can write a good introduction that describes the papers well.

As shown in Figure 1, The model follows like " “Establishing the territory” the author tries to give a basic introduction about the topic. In the second one, “Establishing the niche” the author finds a gap in a certain topic which he or she takes as an initial motivation and tries to resolve through the research. The last one, “Occupying the niche”, is an important part of the CARS model, where the author tries to submit all the findings that he or she has done [3]."

2.2. Move 1 : Establishing the territory

"In this move, the author sets the context for his or her research, providing the necessary background on the topic [3]." There are three steps to satisfy the move.

2.2.1 Step 1 : Claiming centrality

As readers are also the writers they will be able to understand the domain which they are looking for by mentioni ng the centrality of the niche, so it's easy for them to categorise their research in a certain way. So as a writer it's important to mention under what domain the paper will fit in.

Examples:

" Recently there has been a spate of interest in ... [3] ".

2.2.2 Step 2 : Making topic generalisation

During the research process or in some time due to the problems they found in certain areas, writers write papers by highlighting that particular problem which will normally convert into the topic.

Examples:

" Properties of X are still not completely understood [3] " .

2.2.3 Step 3 : Reviewing Previous Items of Research

As this is a common step in almost every scientific paper, to find out to what extent that domain has been explored, we need previous research papers. By mentioning them the readers can widen their knowledge on the particular problem.

Examples:

" Both Johnson and Morgan claim that the biographical facts have been misrepresented [3] " .

2.3. Move 2 : Establishing the Niche

"In this move, the author argues that there is an open "niche" in the existing research, a space that needs to be filled through additional research. The author can establish a niche in one of the four ways [3] ".

So a beginner who is writing scientific papers, it's important to clearly describe our target audience and how we proceed with our findings. In a writer's point of view, giving a strong opinion on a topic is important, which adds value to the paper during the publishing.

2.3.1 Step 1A : Counter-claiming

As a writer as well as reader it will be helpful for them to make clear understanding of a particular topic which they are looking for , so by doing that they can clearly point out the previous research or an followed method is indicating something wrong about a certain topic.

Examples:

" While Jones and Riley believe X method to be accurate, a close examination demonstrates their method to be flawed [3] ".

2.3.2 Step 1B : Indicating a Gap

So in this particular move, we came to understand that the writer describes the gap he or she found during the research, but not mentioning how to tackle the problem, which will be followed in the later section.

Examples:

" While existing studies have clearly established X, they have not addressed Y [3] ".

2.3.3 Step 1C : Question-raising

The main difference between Step 1 and Step 3 is that Step 1 describes the problem which should be rectified, but Step 3 explains the unclear situation about a certain niche.

Examples:

" While Jones and Morgan have established X, these findings raise a number of questions, including ... [3] ".

2.3.4 Step 1D : Continuing a tradition

"The paper tries to follow the same pattern as the rest of the paper with an improved result at the end. [3]". So most of the papers in a scientific section we are able to see following this tradition.

Examples:

" Earlier studies seemed to suggest X. To verify this finding, more work is urgently needed [3] ".

2.4 Move 3 : Occupying the niche

The main difference between move 2 and move 3 is that the author shows some proof which he or she found after the research as an end result.

This is a crucial part of structuring a paper's introduction, because in move 2, we are providing our concrete view to write a paper. But through move 3 we confirm the readers that our claims are true, by giving the proof through our research.

There are four ways to Occupy a niche according to CARS model,

2.4.1 Step lA : Outlining Purposes

" In this section the author provides the theme of the paper which he or she found, but not exactly the result of the paper [3]".

Examples:

" In this article I argue ... [3] ".

2.4.2 Step lB : Announcing present research

"The author describes the research in the current article [3]"

Examples:

" This paper describes three separate studies conducted between March 2008 and January 2009. [3] ".

2.4.3 Step 2 : Announcing principle findings

This section will come under the scope in some papers as in this section the author will mention to what extent the paper will cover, so the readers will get an early preview of the paper.

Examples:

" The results of the study suggest ... [3] ".

2.4.4 Step 3 : Indicating RA structure

" This section will come under the scope in some papers as in this section the author will mention to what extent the paper will cover [4] ", so the readers will get an early preview of the paper.

Examples:

" This paper is structured as follows ... [3] ".
